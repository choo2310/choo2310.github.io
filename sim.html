Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF)

Tyler Choo

The ICAO 2050 Mission

The second conference on aviation and alternative fuels (CAAF/2) endorsed the 2050 ICAO Vision for Sustainable Aviation Fuels as a living inspirational path and called on States, industry and other stakeholders, for a significant proportion of conventional aviation fuels (CAF) to be substituted with sustainable aviation fuels (SAF) by 2050, for international civil aviation to reduce carbon emissions significantly, and whilst pursuing all opportunities in the basket of mitigation measures to reduce emissions as necessary.

The Solution

Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF)

SAF is aviation fuel synthesized from renewable resources. The main source of SAF is currently biofuel, otherwise known as aviation biofuel. The sources of SAF are varied, and many are still being tested.

Sources of Aviation Biofuel

There are 3 main emerging sources for SAF, and 1 already commercialized option: HEFA-SPK

Of the 10 proposed sources for aviation biofuel, 2 are in prototype, 7 are being demonstrated, and 1 is already in use commercially.

How much do we need?

The ICAO estimates that we will need 1 billion dry-tonnes of biomass to produce 50 billion gallons of sustainable aviation fuel.

Rolls Royce began testing 100% aviation biofuel on their engine test platforms in early 2021.

The Benefits

Cleaner Skies

In 2018, NASA conducted a series of tests and found that just a 50/50 blend of biofuel and conventional fuel produced up to 70% reduction in soot emissions from commercial jet engines.

The use of biofuels will also reduce toxic gas emissions from low-flying aircraft, improving the health of near-airport neighborhoods.

A NASA DC-8 flying on a 50/50 biofuel blend.

Other Benefits

The use of aviation biofuels also has the potenetial to boost revenue to farmers who grow the necessary plants to produce aviation biofuels.

The construction of more biofuel factories will also create more jobs.

Going Carbon Neutral

The plants grown to produce biofuels will also absorb oxygen. The ICAO hopes that at large enough production scales, the absorption of CO2 will be enough to offset the emissions by aircraft. The IATA predicts an 80% reduction in lifecycle carbon emissions.

In 2008, Virgin Atlantic flew the first commercial flight powered by a biofuel blend.

The Journey

Governments need to help

The Biden administration has committed 4.3 billion US dollars to SAF R&D in order to achieve at least a 30% improvement in fuel efficiency.

It's booming!

The aviation biofuel industry is expected to grow 77x from 2020 to 2028.

Boeing's fleet of ecoDemonstrator aircraft have been using various types SAF as part of their testing for a more sustainable future.

The Challenges

High Demand

In order to produce enough biofuel to meet global demands for aviation fuel, we will not be able to rely on just one production method. Multiple processes of aviation biofuel development will need to be developed.

Drop In

In order to quickly substitute SAF for current conventional fuels, the replacement must be a drop-in replacement. SAF must work with existing engines.

Because SAF is planned to work with existing engines and technology, they must also be able to meet all current safety and regulatory requirements that conventional aviation fuel meets.

Really Eco-friendly?

Testing has shown burning SAF has little reduction in CO2 emissions. This means that carbon neutrality must be obtained by offsetting the production. This is done mainly through CO2 absorption by the plants grown to produce the biofuels.

Gulfstream began using SAF in 2016 for their daily operations, including in flight tests of their new aircraft.

Who are Helping?

Airlines considered pioneers of SAF

Airlines considered leading contributors to sustainability

Almost all of the large aircraft producers in the world have committed to using SAF and developing aircraft capable of being fueled with 100% biofuel blends in the future.
